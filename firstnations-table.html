Northwest Coast
  • lots of different languages
    • five main groups: Haida, Tlingit, Tsimshain - Nishga - Gitksan, Nuu-cha-nulth - Haisla - Heiltsuk - Kwakwakw’wakw, and Salishan
  • many different groups as well
    • main: Haida, Tlingit, Tsimshian, Nuu-chah-nulth, Salishan
  • by the coast of North America (from Washington state -> Alaska)
  • lived near water + relied on it
  • forests, used cedar trees for basically everything
    • houses, canoes, clothes, etc
  • had permanent settlements: longhouses made of cedar
    • 50-150 feet long
    • held several families
    • no windows, just smokehole at top
    • like huge rectangle-boxes
  • had villages with multiple longhouses
    • totem poles marked villages; also had house poles
 
  • didn’t need agriculture
    • hunter-gatherers
  • salmon = most important food
    • used nets/harpoons
    • lots of fishing
      • could get oil from Eulachon fish
  • also had things like crab/clams/shellfish
    • collected by women/slaves
  • whales hunted, although dangerous to do so
  • deer/elk/mountain goat from forest eaten
  • meat was boiled/baked/steamed using wood boxes, hot rocks, and water
  • could eat fish fresh/dried
  • tools were cedar, stone, or shell
    • hammers, bows, nets/fishing lines, bowls
 
  • made canoes out of cedar trees
    • large + long, up to 50 feet long/8 feet wide
    • held 2-50 people, 10,000lbs of cargo
  • don’t migrate/move; more sedentary
  • sometimes moved to temporary camps to fish during summer
    • only short periods though
 
  • believed that spirits interfered with natural world
    • only shamans could interact with them
      • could go into trance to communicate
      • cured ill, etc
    • used rattles, dances to summon spirits
  • had potlatches (big celebrations with feasts + gift giving)
    • held for building of new house, birth, marriage, offset of humiliation, etc
  • had art like baskets, woodcarving (totems), bentwood boxes, weaving
  • clothes were made of leather/bark
    • although didn’t wear most of the time (only when it was cold)
    • had bark capes/hats for protection against rain
    • also wore colourful blankets (chilkat blanket)
 
  • specific roles for men/women
    • men hunted/fished, built, carved
    • women did chores, cooking, clothes-making, weaving
  • marriages between clans
    • man paid wife’s family, after having a child, wife’s family paid man
      • then marriage could be split if they wanted to
  • had a kind of “nobility-based” structure
    • wealthiest people at top, several classes
    • weather distributed according to class
    • Chief was oldest person that was closest to common ancestor of family
  • had clans, inherited through mother’s side of family
    • displayed through crests/canoes/poles etc
 		  
  • groups traded a lot
  • meetings set up to trade
    • currency: blankets
    • often traded oil
  • did have some disagreements, with some wars
    • wars used to get property (land, blankets, copper slaves, etc)
    • not a lot of war, because gift-giving solved problems 
Plateau  
  • have three main language groups: Athapascan, Interior Salishan, and Ktunaxa
  • eight main groups: Tsilhqot’in, Carrier, Nicola, Secwepemc, Stl’atl’imx, Okanagan, Nlaka’pamux, Ktunaxa
  
  • area of BC between coast/Rockies
  • has waterways (esp. lakes) + forests
  • rivers were important because salmon
    • Thompson, Kootenay, Columbia rivers
  • semi-nomadic; moved for food
    • pit house = mostly underground shelter, used mostly in winter
    • tipi/tule-mat lodge = wooden frame “tents” covered with reed mats, used in summer
    • lean-to = very temporary shelters similar to tipis
  • had seasonal villages
    • looser villages/clusters in summer, more permanent/bigger during winter
  
  • mostly hunter-gatherers
  • hunted deer/caribou, more experienced hunters killed bear/goat
  • also hunted coyotes, foxes, racoons, hares, etc
  • salmon played a big role
    • fished using traps, nets, spears, baskets
    • mostly shared caught fish, unless using private fishing ground/only getting a few
  • women prepared food by drying/smoking/roasting/boiling
  • used bone/wood/stone tools
    • arrows, nets, spears, ladles, etc
  • used canoes (sturgeon-nosed)
  • made from trees, with lacing of deer/moose/caribou hide
  • also used horses, dogs, snowshoes
  • semi-nomadic; followed food patterns
 
  • believed that there were spirits for everything
    • some good, some bad
  • respected animals
  • had shamans who could communicate with spirit world
  • had myths/legends involving creator “Coyote”
  • several ceremonies, including start of spring
    • instruments, dancing, singing
    • spirit dance, sun dance
  • in terms of art, had rock painting/weaving/carving
  • clothing was made out of bark/grass/hides
    • had dresses/shirts/skirts, ponchos/capes, mittens/moccasins
    • had headdresses, to show status
 		  
  • lived in family groups
  • men made decisions, hunted/fished/trapped, fought in war
  • women prepared food, made clothing, cared for children
  • no real social classes
    • most things were communal, decisions democratized
    • there were chiefs for certain roles (like hunting, war, head of village)
    • sometimes had council of Elders
  
  • mostly peaceful
    • HOWEVER: sometimes war over food
  • used spears/knives/clubs to fight
  • sometimes built stockades during extended wars
Plains  
  • 3 languages: Algonquian, Athapascan, Siouan
  • 4 groups: Blackfoot Confederacy, Plains Cree, Plains Sioux, and Plains Ojibwa
    • Blackfoot Confederacy = Kainai, Siksika, Pikuni, Sarcee
    • Sioux = Nakoda, Assiniboine
  
  • east = high grass on prairies, west = short grass
  • extreme weather (-40º -> 35º)
  • some rivers
  • big herds of deer/buffalo/antelope + bears, etc
    • also some small animals like prairie dogs/rabbits
  • had bands of people in tipis
    • like cone tents, with pine poles/hides for side
      • had smoke hole + stones to keep it down during wind
    • often had designs on tipi
    • women set up tipis
  
  • mostly buffalo (dried/cooked/soups/pemmican)
  • also ate berries, deer/moose, prairie chickens, etc
  • bannock = bread cooked over fire
  • used bows/spears to hunt buffalos
    • could also make them stampede off cliff
  • used traps for wolves, coyotes, etc
  • tools used include shields of hide, tomahawks, coup sticks
  • by foot; used snowshoes/dogsleds during winter
    • travois = wood frame structure on dogs for transport
  • had horses later, which allowed more possessions/less travel
    • food could be brought from farther away
  • migrated through certain areas where they knew there was food
    • most bands stayed separate
      • grouped to do buffalo jumps in summer
 
  • believed one great god “Wakan Tanka” (Great Spirit)
  • blessed people = shamans, could heal
  • everything had spirits, Earth was mother of spirits
  • had ceremonies for spiritual stuff
    • vision quest where young men fasted for visions
    • sun dance responding to vision/spirits
  • art included paintings on tipis, quillwork (like embroidery)
  • clothing included dresses/shirts, ponchos, moccasins, etc
 		  
  • men hunted, women cooked/made clothing/cared for children
    • women also built tipis
  • children cared for by whole village
    • sat in moss bags (like diapers)
    • cradle boards allowed them to be carried on mother’s back
  
  • every tribe respected hunting ground of others
  • there were some alliances/rivalries
    • blackfoot confederacy = one big alliance
    • Assiniboine + Cree = alliance
  • war = sending some warriors to surprise tribe
    • warriors left as if they wouldn’t return
Subartic  
  • has 2 main languages: Algonquian and Athapascan
    • HOWEVER: lots of different dialects, some highly unique
  • groups: the Dene, Tagish, Tahltan, Kaska, Sekani, Tsetsaut, Gwich’in, Han, Tutchones, Northern Ojibwa, Attikamek, Innu
    • Dene = Dogrib, Hare, Mountain, Slavey, Beaver, and Chipewyan nations
  • forested area, along with some mountain ranges and poor/swampy soil
  • winters - long + harsh
  • moose, bear, sheep, etc
  • wolf, weasel, coyote, etc
  • lots of fish
  • some migratory birds
  • in terms of housing, there were double lean-tos and outhouses, along with wigwams
 
  • hunting/fishing/trapping + gathering plants
    • could go ice fishing + get fish eggs
  • berries + ox/elk/bear meat could be used for pemmican
  • men hunted big game, women snared/processed
    • lots of effort to hunt caribou
  • tools used include bows, sleds, nets, spears, knives, etc
 		  
  • mostly by foot; had snowshoes/dogsleds for transport
  • groups (bands or people) moved around frequently
    • followed food source
    • traveled light
      • no real “ownership”
    • people switched between bands frequently
  
  • hero/trickster = Nanabush/Wisahkecahk in Algonquian culture
  • had heroes/spirits
  • There were medicine people, with abilities to heal
  • had divination based on caribou shoulder blade cracking over fires
  • art included singing/dancing, narratives, clothing decoration
  • had guessing games
  • clothing included hide moccasins, leggings, shirts, coats
    • usually wore lighter, built fire if cold
    • had sleeping robes for winter time
  • family unit = highly respected
  • families grouped together for hunting/ceremonies
  • for some, clans grouped matriarchially
  • sometimes men had multiple wives
  • leadership was kind of natural; people with ability to lead + initiative were followed
    • most adults could have a say
    • HOWEVER: some leaders more forceful
    • families/people disagreeing with decisions could leave
  • bands could band up, formed regional bands
    • good for hunting
 		  
  • groups speaking Algonquian teamed up
  • there were some wars between Innu + others, and between Chipewyan + Cree
    • also between Chipewyan/Cree and Inuit/Dene
  • not that much war though
Inuit  
  • many different dialects/languages; however, all come from same family
  • Inuktitut and Yupik are main
    • have other names
    • dialects across different regions
  • have eight regions
    • Labrador, Ungava/New Quebec, Baffin Island, Igloolik, Caribou, Netsilik, Copper, Western Arctic
  
  • harsh, cold Arctic
  • ice and snow everywhere, almost year-round winter
  • sometimes complete darkness (no sun) or complete light (never fully set), windstorms
  • basically nothing grew, even during summer
  • nomadic living conditions
    • lived in caribou/seal skin tents during summer
    • lived in igloos during winter
  • Western Arctic group had wood, so could build partially underground wood forts
  • sometimes formed bigger villages during summer
  • always small groups in winter
  
  • mainly hunted; relied on Arctic animals for food
    • couldn’t really depend on plants
  • ice fishing, harpoon hunting at holes in ice, harpoons on open water, kayaking + fishing
  • usually ate meat raw, but could cook (uncommon) or dry/freeze
  • common meats: caribou, seal/walrus, beluga/narwhal, musk oxen, arctic hares
  • used stone/bone/ivory/antler/tooth tools
  • had harpoons, spears, fishing lines, bow/arrows, and knives
  • foot, dogsled, boat
  • used crampons to walk on ice
  • had kayaks/umiaks for hunting/travel
  • moved around following animals (food source)
 		  
  • believed that everything had a spirit, even inanimate objects
  • had shamans called “angakoks”
    • could communicate to spirit world
  • had certain practices to appease spirits
    • EXAMPLE: saved bladder of animals and did rituals before putting back into sea
  • had art; important in culture
    • carvings of stone/bone/ivory
    • masks of whalebone/wood
    • inukshuks
  • clothing was animal skins/fur
    • many layers (obviously)
    • could also use intestines because more waterproof
  • had sealskin boots/fur slippers
  
  • family unit = most important in culture
    • 5-6 people
  • family would band up with others
  • men built houses + got food
  • women cooked/dressed skins + made clothes + looked after children
  • no leader/Chief
    • loose groups/alliances, community-style living
  
  • good relations
  • shared a lot
  • respected each other, very peaceful
  • rarely conflict/war between groups
Eastern Woodland Hunters  
  • two languages: Central Algonquian + Eastern Algonquian
  • groups: Ojibwa, Ottawa, Nipissing, Algonquin, Maliseet, Mi’kmaq
  
  • pretty diverse
    • forests, coastal regions, lowlands, Appalachians, etc
  • lots of birch trees
    • used to build canoes, baskets, wigwams, etc
  • several adventageous water features
    • St. Lawrence River, Great Lakes, Atlantic Coast + more
  • semi-nomadic; formed villages sometimes, stuck to small bands mostly
  • lived in wigwams
    • dome-shaped “tents”, one per family
    • could also be cone-shaped like a tipi
    • built using wooden frame + overlapping parts
  
  • hunter-gatherers
    • didn’t rely on growing, but some groups did farm
      • EXAMPLE: Mi’kmaq grew tobacco
  • were good at fishing, because had lots of water nearby
    • sometimes built villages closer to fishing places
    • also caught eels, crustaceans
  • most important prey = white-tailed deer
    • meat could be eaten, hide used for house/clothing
    • also hunted squirrel, reacoon, seal, whale, etc
  • hunted different animals depending on area
    • EXAMPLE: coastal people = hunt seal, northern people = hunt caribou
  • cooking = roasting/boiling, smoke/dry
  • tools = wood/bark
    • spears, bows, nets, hooks, etc
  
  • lots of water, so could paddle canoes
    • made of birch bark (light + strong)
  • traveled on land during winter
    • sleds/tobaggans, snowshoes
      • different types if carrying more/less stuff
  • nomadic, moved to follow food source
    • moved less in summer, but switched camps in fall before winter set in
  • believed in spirit world constantly interacting, and one big one called “Kitchie Manitou” (Great Spirit)
  • Shamans (medicine men) could talk to/control spirits
    • had rituals to ward off evil spirits
    • powerful leaders in culture
  • had “Windigo” (evil spirit) which were people lost in wood who turned into cannibals
    • had to kill host + burn body to destory spirit
    • could be host of a Windigo by dreaming of it/being bitten/being cursed by shaman
  • ceremonies = changing seasons, birth, death, puberty
    • used facepaint + masks + bead belts (wampum)
  • art = beadwork + quills, dreamcatchers, decorative clothing
  • clothing = hide robes/leggings/moccasins, coats, mittens, wampum belts
 		  
  • men hunted/fished, women farmed/gathered
    • women also got things like wild rice, maple sap
    • women also set up wigwams + cared for children
  • no big alliances between groups
  • had Chiefs for village-bands (groups of families)
  • had clans passed patriarchally, symbolized by animals
  
  • quite a bit of trade; beaver fur, fish, corn, etc
  • not realy involved in war
    • had some disagreements between/within tribes
      • HOWEVER: no escalation into big “war”
  • different groups (village-bands) had different relations
    • relationship with neighbouring nations (outside Eastern Woodland Hunters) varied
Eastern Wooodland Farmers  
  • one main language: Iroquoian
  • groups: Huron (orginally Wyandot/Wendat), Neutrals, Petun/Tobacco, Iroquois
  • small area, no specialized dialects or more groups
  
  • lots of trees; maples super important because syrup
    • elm also important to make house/canoe
  • had a few lakes/rivers/streams
  • had squirrels/rabbits, bears/moose/deer, etc
  • didn’t really have to move around as environment was good
    • developed agriculture
  • created villages every 10-30 years
    • after a while soil depleted, place became dirty, less prey, etc
    • usually placed in partially cleared forest
  • built longhouses for village
    • long homes, like giant upside-down baskets
    • usually divided into 20ft sections, could fit multiple families
  
  • eveloped agriculture
    • grew “Three Sisters”: corn, bean, squash
      • worked together to help each other grow
    • slash & burned trees for farmland
      • circular fields
  • women grew the plants
    • done to bring good luck
    • sometimes stood in fields as live scarecrows
  • harvested a lot of corn, up to 150,000 bushels/season
    • also grew berries/tubers/herbs/tobacco
  • more stable lifestyle = able to make grinding stones for corn
  • hunted sometimes for deer, fish, etc
  • food was stored in pot in longhouse (help-yourself model)
  • tools = stone axes, arrowheads, knives
  • mainly on foot/by canoe
  • used snowshoes during winter
    • tree branch + animal sinew
    • could jump-scare animals stuck in snow
  • canoes = bent bark
  • migration uncommon
    • villages last for long times
    • women stayed there, men sometimes went out to hunt/trap
 		  
  • oral tradition
    • stories passed doewn over generations
    • nothing written down
  • different myths/purposes = different purpose/meaning
    • symbolism used often
  • had different spirits for natural things (trees, animals, moon, etc)
    • could pray to these for help
    • could interpret dreams as guidance
  • spirit doctors (shamans) helped with religious matters, as well as False Face Society
    • healers using spiritual masks
  • had six ceremonies per year: New Year, Maple, Corn Planting, trawberry, Green Corn, and Harvest
    • 4 of 6 = corn-focused
  • clothing = fur/hide, cornhusk, plant fiber
    • moccassins, kastoweh (ceremonial hat)
    • had leather kilts/leggings + winter shirts for men, depending on season
    • had dresses with leggings for women
  
  • families important, extended shared living conditions
    • elders respected; oldest female = master of longhouse
    • eldest fathers = storytellers
  • female = raised children + cared for fields; male = hunters, protectors, warriors, builders
    • children also helped out with everyday tasks
  • villages belong to clan/tribe, collection of villages = Nation
    • villages have sachem (chief) for guidance/law enforcement
    • also had warrior chief (position gained through bravery/wealth)
  • status = how much stuff can you give to other people?
    • increased people’s reliance on each other + strengthened community
  • public confession used to restore peace when wrongdoing occured
    • if conflict between nations/tribes, councils tried to settle things
    • if murder, male members of victim’s longhouse honour-bound to kill murderer
  • quite a history of war
  • 1000 years ago = war broke out when Huran man killed Seneca chief
    • Iroquois exiled
  • eventually Iroquois teamed up as a five-nation alliance
    • attacked other Eastern Woodland Farmers
    • were successful in conquering + taking over land
